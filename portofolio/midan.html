Case Study — Talent Management Platform

Midan

A comprehensive, multi-sided digital ecosystem for Al Wehda Club — one platform where coaches, players, and administrators each operate from an interface tuned to their role.

01 — The Ecosystem Context

One club, several interlocking workflows.

A club's talent operation isn't a single workflow — it's several that constantly feed one another. A coach's evaluation becomes a player's development plan, which becomes an administrator's record. When those functions live in disconnected spreadsheets and single-purpose tools, the picture fragments: data is duplicated, context is lost, and no one sees the whole.

Midan's mandate was to replace that fragmentation with a single, coherent multi-sided ecosystem — a shared source of truth that still presents each participant with only what is relevant to them. The design challenge was not one product, but three tightly coupled experiences that must stay consistent without collapsing into a one-size-fits-all interface.

02 — Understanding the Users

Three roles, three mental models.

Through interviews, role modeling, and journey mapping across the club, I defined each side of the ecosystem by the job it needed to do — and, critically, where those jobs shared data and where they diverged.

Coaches

Evaluate & develop

Assess performance, build training plans, track progress over time, and deliver structured, comparable feedback — quickly, often pitch-side.

Players

See & grow

Understand their development path, feedback, goals, and schedule with clarity and agency — motivation sustained by visible progress.

Administrators

Oversee & govern

Maintain oversight, data integrity, permissions, and reporting across the whole operation, with the controls governance demands.

03 — System Architecture

One source of truth, many lenses.

The architecture rests on a shared data model governed by role-based access control. Every participant reads from and writes to the same underlying record, but each role sees a distinct, purpose-built lens over it — eliminating duplication while preserving a single, trustworthy truth.

  • Role-tuned dashboards. Each role's home surfaces only what is actionable for them, cutting cognitive load and decision friction.
  • Permission & governance layers. Sensitive data and administrative controls are gated by role, satisfying the club's oversight requirements without cluttering everyday use.
  • Consistency for learnability. Shared components and patterns keep the system coherent across roles; interfaces diverge only where the job genuinely demands it.
  • Engineered engagement. Applying Fogg's Behavior Model (B = MAP), I placed timely prompts where motivation and ability meet — nudging players to log progress and coaches to close feedback loops, so the platform stays alive with current data.

04 — Inclusive Design

Usable by everyone in the ecosystem.

Midan's users span a wide range of ability, age, and digital literacy — from veteran administrators to young athletes. Inclusive design wasn't a final pass; it shaped the system from the start.

  • Arabic-first & RTL-aware. Layouts, components, and typography were designed for right-to-left reading and bilingual use, not retrofitted onto a left-to-right assumption.
  • Range of digital literacy. Plain language, progressive disclosure, and forgiving, recoverable flows keep the platform approachable for first-time and expert users alike.
  • WCAG-aligned accessibility. Sufficient contrast, clear focus states, adequate touch targets, and keyboard operability are built into the component library.
  • Real contexts of use. Interfaces were designed for how they're actually used — a coach on a phone at the side of the pitch, an admin at a desk — across devices and conditions.
