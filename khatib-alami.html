Case Study — Corporate Website Redesign

Khatib & Alami

Rebuilding first-impression credibility for one of the region's largest engineering and architecture firms — a strategic redesign of its corporate website, delivered as a fully interactive proof-of-concept.

01 — Executive Summary

Credibility, made legible in seconds.

Khatib & Alami has spent over five decades shaping landmark infrastructure across the region — including design development and supervision on Dubai's Cayan Tower, the world's tallest twisted tower at its completion. Yet its corporate website communicated none of that authority. During my internship, I identified the gap between the firm's real-world stature and its digital first impression, proposed a full redesign on my own initiative, pitched the idea to stakeholders, and led the team to redesign it.

The result is a modernized, highly practical, and frictionless corporate website that elevates the holistic user experience. While building client trust was a primary goal, the redesign fundamentally transformed the entire user journey through persuasive and behavioral design principles. The legacy site severely violated Hick’s Law by overwhelming users with fragmented, org-chart-driven choices — forcing them to parse between ‘Who We Are’, ‘What We Do’, ‘Our CEO’, ‘Our Board of Directors’, and ‘Our People’. We eliminated this cognitive overload by compressing the navigation into a clear, intuitive path of four purposeful destinations (unifying all corporate context seamlessly under ‘About Us’).

Beyond structural navigation, we replaced a dead-end homepage with a five-section storytelling scroll for a modern, practical presentation. To maximize usability and inclusivity, we also integrated dedicated accessibility controls — specifically Dark Mode and dynamic text scaling (zoom in/out) — ensuring an inclusive, adaptable interface for all users.

Business value in one line: A holistic UX transformation that delivers a modern, accessible, and persuasive corporate journey, instantly building client trust before a conversation ever begins.

02 — The Problem

A monumental firm, undersold by its own site.

Legacy corporate websites in the engineering sector share a predictable failure pattern: they are structured around the organization's internal logic rather than the visitor's decision-making process. The existing site was a textbook case. An expert audit surfaced five compounding problems:

  • Navigation that mirrored the org chart, not the visitor's questions. The primary menu carried eleven top-level items — arbitrarily separating “Our CEO”, “Our Board of Directors”, and “Our People” into three distinct destinations — plus a persistent “Employee Area” button in the most prominent slot. Leadership biographies held three times the navigational real estate of the firm's actual capabilities. Per Hick's Law, every added option inflates decision time.
  • A homepage that was a dead end. The entire page consisted of a full-screen image slider with no scrollable content beneath it. A visitor who did not immediately dive into the eleven-item menu had nowhere to go: no narrative, no proof, no pathway.
  • A hero that never said what the firm does. The slider opened on a striking Dubai skyline captioned in a low-contrast, all-caps overlay — “The World's Tallest Twisted Tower – 2014”. Impressive raw material, fatally framed: visitors were shown a building before being told this firm engineers buildings like this. There was no value proposition and no call to action anywhere on the page.
  • A one-size-fits-all journey. Prospective clients, partners, and candidates were funneled through the same undifferentiated path — no journey was designed for anyone in particular, so it served no one well.
  • Eroded perceived quality — and a language gap. An early-2010s visual language quietly contradicted the excellence of the practice behind it, and the experience led with English in a market where Arabic-first communication is itself a trust signal. Users judge trustworthiness substantially on visual design quality — and they do it in under a second.

03 — The Process

Diagnose with evidence, not opinion.

Heuristic evaluation. I began with a structured expert review against Nielsen's ten usability heuristics, documenting violations and their severity: top-level labels that reflected internal org structure rather than visitor vocabulary (match between system and the real world); eleven competing options and a single-project hero (aesthetic and minimalist design); no persistent placement for the content that drives decisions (recognition rather than recall); and inconsistent typography and components (consistency and standards). The output was a prioritized defect map — a ranked inventory of friction, ordered by impact on trust formation.

Competitive & comparative analysis. I benchmarked leading global and regional engineering, architecture, and consulting firms to map category conventions visitors expect (capability-first homepages, sector-based wayfinding, project-led storytelling), the trust toolkit top firms deploy (quantified scale, hero imagery of built work used as evidence), and the differentiation gap: most regional competitors also underperformed digitally — a credibility-first, Arabic-first redesign wouldn't just reach parity; it would create separation.

Information architecture restructuring. A content audit ranked every item against one criterion: does this move a decision-maker toward trust? The eleven-item menu collapsed into four intent-driven destinations — Home, About, Projects, Contact — plus a clean language switcher. Leadership content merged into About; the Employee Area left the public journey entirely; the standalone “360° Perspective” link was reconceived as immersive content within the story rather than a dead-end menu item. The homepage itself became a scrollable journey of five well-paced sections governed by progressive disclosure and a deliberate narrative arc: Capability → Proof → Contact.

The findings converged on three principles that governed every subsequent decision: clarity, credibility, and hierarchy.

04 — Design Decisions

Every choice, in service of trust.

An identity-first hero. The redesigned hero replaces the project-caption banner with a structured statement of identity: a positioning badge (ريادة في الهندسة — “Leadership in Engineering”), the firm's name and discipline in a confident display scale, and one quantified proof line — 50+ years of innovation and planning, the number accented in brand orange so the eye cannot miss the firm's strongest credential. Within seconds, a visitor can answer both questions the legacy site left open: what does this firm do, and can I trust them?

Persuasion architecture — Fogg's Behavior Model. A qualified inquiry happens when motivation, ability, and a prompt converge. Motivation: proof is front-loaded — the 50-year credential in the hero, a floating experience badge in About, flagship projects on the homepage. Ability: a four-item IA and scannable sections make the path to any answer short and obvious. Prompt: a two-tier call to action matched to visitor readiness — a primary WhatsApp “Inquire Now” (استفسر الآن), deliberately the lowest-friction, culturally native business channel in the region, and a secondary “Explore Our Projects” (استكشف مشاريعنا) for visitors who need more proof first. High-intent and still-evaluating visitors each get a next step; the legacy site gave neither one.

The storytelling scroll. Where the legacy homepage ended at the slider, the redesign unfolds as a narrative in five paced sections: the hero flows into an asymmetric About section — editorial headline set against an elevated workplace photograph anchored by the floating “+50 years of experience” stat badge — then a card-based Featured Projects grid that chunks the portfolio into scannable units. Midway down, the palette deliberately inverts: a dark-themed 360° VR section creates a visual break that resets attention exactly where scroll fatigue would set in, before the journey closes in a structured multi-column footer. Progressive disclosure, applied to visual rhythm as much as content depth.

Proof as wayfinding. Featured projects — Jabal Omar, Najran Airport, the Riyadh services program, SAR GIS — are presented as sector-labeled cards (architecture, infrastructure, e-services), doing double duty as evidence of scale and navigational scent. The buried “360° Perspective” menu item was reborn inside the story: VR walkthroughs of real sites with explicit play affordances, turning a dead link into one of the experience's most engaging trust devices.

Accessibility and localization as baseline. The redesign is Arabic-first with native RTL typography and a one-click English switcher — matching the firm's primary market rather than defaulting to English. A floating accessibility widget travels with the user — text zoom and contrast/dark-mode controls — alongside WCAG-aligned contrast and fully responsive layouts. The brand orange is retained from the firm's existing logo: modernizing the identity without severing recognition.

05 — The Outcome

A working proof-of-concept, not a promise.

The concept was developed into a fully interactive, high-fidelity demo hosted on GitHub Pages — a working proof-of-concept rather than static mockups. It validates the complete strategy end-to-end: the four-item information architecture, the capability-proof-contact journey, the bilingual RTL experience, and the premium visual system, all experienced as a real, navigable product.

  • It makes the strategic argument tangible — stakeholders don't have to imagine the redesign; they can click through it, switch languages, and toggle dark mode.
  • It demonstrates end-to-end ownership — from identifying the problem unprompted, through the pitch, to a shipped interactive artifact.
  • It establishes the baseline for validation — the natural next step is moderated usability testing and first-click / time-to-information measurement against the legacy site, for which the demo serves as the test artifact.

This project sharpened a conviction I bring to every engagement: in professional services, the interface is the first proof of competence a client ever sees. The legacy site owned extraordinary raw material — the world's tallest twisted tower was literally on its homepage — and still failed to convert it into trust, because credibility is not what you show; it's what the visitor can effortlessly understand.
